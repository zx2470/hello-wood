The Orinoco Mining Arc in Venezuela is a large-scale mining project that has raised concerns due to its significant impact on natural ecosystems and indigenous communities. This initiative, spanning an area rich in biodiversity, has led to deforestation, habitat destruction, and contamination of water sources. Protected areas and watersheds are particularly affected, disrupting delicate ecological balances. Indigenous people, who rely on the land for their livelihoods, face displacement and the loss of their traditional way of life. Additionally, the mining arc has witnessed illegal mining activities, further exacerbating environmental degradation and posing serious threats to both the environment and the well-being of local communities. This webpage aims to highlight these issues, shedding light on the consequences of the mining arc on protected areas, watersheds, and the indigenous population.

The jaguar (Panthera onca) is the largest big cat native to the Americas and is known for its powerful build and distinctive spotted coat. Jaguars face various threats, including habitat loss, poaching, and conflicts with humans. Their conservation status varies across different regions, but overall, jaguars are listed as "Near Threatened" on the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Red List.
Canaima National Park, located in Venezuela, is a vast and ecologically diverse area that is home to a variety of wildlife, including jaguars. Conservation efforts in the park are crucial to protecting the biodiversity of the region.

Protected Areas Canaima National Park Endangered Giant Otter

The giant otter (Pteronura brasiliensis) is the largest of the otter species and is found in various freshwater habitats, including rivers, lakes, and swamps, in South America. These charismatic animals are known for their playful behavior, close family bonds, and distinctive vocalizations.
Giant otters face several threats, including habitat destruction, pollution, and conflicts with humans. They are listed as "Endangered" on the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Red List. The primary threats to their survival include habitat loss due to deforestation, water pollution from mining and agriculture, and direct hunting for their pelts.

Protected Areas Canaima National Park Endangered Giant Anteater

The giant anteater (Myrmecophaga tridactyla) is a unique and distinctive mammal found in Central and South America, including parts of Venezuela. These animals are well-adapted to their diet, which primarily consists of ants and termites. Giant anteaters are known for their long snouts, bushy tails, and distinctive black and white fur patterns.
Giant anteaters face threats that are commonly associated with habitat loss, including deforestation, agricultural expansion, and infrastructure development. Additionally, they can be vulnerable to threats such as wildfires, vehicle collisions, and hunting.
The conservation status of giant anteaters is categorized as "Vulnerable" on the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Red List. This designation implies that the species faces a high risk of extinction in the wild if the threats persist.

Protected Areas Imataca Forest Reserve Mining Pollution

Imataca Forest Reserve is a protected area located in Venezuela, known for its rich biodiversity and extensive rainforest. The reserve is home to various species of flora and fauna, and it plays a crucial role in maintaining ecological balance.
However, mining activities, especially illegal and unregulated ones, can pose significant threats to the environment, including deforestation, habitat destruction, and pollution of air and water. Mining pollution often involves the release of harmful substances such as heavy metals, mercury, and other toxins into water bodies, affecting aquatic ecosystems and posing risks to human health.

Watersheds Mercury Pollution Freshwater Fish Industry

Mercury pollution from gold mining can have significant and far-reaching impacts on freshwater ecosystems and the freshwater fish industry. Mercury is often used in artisanal and small-scale gold mining (ASGM) to extract gold from ore. The process involves amalgamation, where mercury is mixed with gold-containing materials to form a gold-mercury amalgam. Unfortunately, this practice leads to the release of mercury into the environment, with serious consequences for aquatic ecosystems and the organisms that inhabit them, including freshwater fish.

Watersheds Mercury Pollution Dangerous Level of Mercury Exposure to Human

Miners involved in gold mining activities, particularly those using mercury in amalgamation processes, may also face occupational exposure to mercury vapor. This exposure can lead to chronic health problems, including neurological and respiratory issues.

Watersheds Excessive Water Sediment Decline in Hydroelectricity Generation

Excessive sedimentation can reduce the efficiency of hydroelectric power plants. Sediment deposits in reservoirs can decrease the water storage capacity and interfere with the proper functioning of turbines, leading to decreased energy production.
Sediment-laden water can cause wear and tear on the turbines, blades, and other equipment in hydroelectric plants. The abrasive nature of sediment particles can lead to a shorter lifespan for these components, requiring more frequent replacements and repairs.

Watersheds Warmer Water and Increased Flow Atmosphere-Ocean Interaction With Hurricane

The intensity and duration of some of the worst hurricanes in the area are influenced by the interaction of the Orinoco and Amazon Rivers with the atmosphere and ocean. If mining leads to increased discharges from the Orinoco River, it could create more powerful and longer-lasting hurricanes due to warmer waters.

Indigenous People Mercury Exposure Damage

Mercury from mining is getting into the soil and water, exposing local indigenous people to dangerous levels. The Orinoco river, which many indigenous communities rely on for water, is now contaminated with this toxic mercury used in gold extraction. Tests in mining areas of Bolivar state showed over 90% of mine workers had unsafe levels of mercury in their urine, affecting 87% of women and 68% of children. In the Caura river basin, 92% of indigenous women had elevated mercury levels, which can harm the brain, kidneys, and fetal development.

Indigenous People Standing Water Water- and Mosquito-borne Disease

Stagnant water and poor hygiene at mining sites have caused a big increase in water and mosquito-related diseases among the local people. Malaria, a disease that Venezuela had once eliminated, has made a rapid and alarming comeback. In just ten months in 2019, the WHO reported over 323,000 malaria cases in Venezuela, with 10% of the population in Bolivar state testing positive. Malaria is responsible for 21% of deaths in Amazonas state and 25% in Bolivar state. Getting medicine is often tough, but you can buy it with gold on the black market. These conditions have also led to more cases of diphtheria, yellow fever, dengue, and chikungunya.

Indigenous People Repression Displaced People

Indigenous communities resisting illegal mining have faced violent repression and had to flee their ancestral lands.

Indigenous People Forced Labor (including the Underage) Displaced People

Around 500,000 workers, many from local indigenous communities, are in illegal mining. They often feel forced to work due to threats of violence or economic necessity. About 45% of these miners are underage and labor in terrible conditions under the threat of armed groups and gangs.

Indigenous People Sex Trafficking Displaced People

Sex trafficking of women and girls is a major problem. They are either forced into prostitution or do it because they have no other option. Children, especially, are at risk for trafficking, with reports suggesting the average age of victims is just 13-14 years old. The region has seen a rise in sexually transmitted diseases, including HIV/AIDS, due to sexual exploitation.
